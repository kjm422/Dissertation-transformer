\section*{Significance Statement}
\noindent
Using hyperspectral imagery from orbiting remote-sensing platforms to identify surface materials and their abundances requires computationally intensive atmospheric correction and downstream spectral-library matching.
However, a cross-attention spectral transformer trained directly on top-of-atmosphere reflectance can serve as a low-latency surrogate for mineral identification, using a single forward pass.
The architecture creates a transparent path for adding established physical knowledge.
The result is an efficient framework for physics-informed transformers that supports faster ground processing, onboard screening, and revisit decision support for space-based applications.

\section*{Abstract}
\noindent
Hyperspectral imaging spectrometers enable quantitative remote-sensing measurement of surface composition.
Different materials leave distinct signatures in the reflected-light spectrum, from which their identity and abundance can be inferred.
NASA's Earth Surface Mineral Dust Source Investigation (EMIT) maps arid-region surface mineralogy with 285-band imaging spectroscopy across the visible shortwave infrared (VSWIR) range.
Operational data products require atmospheric correction and spectral-library matching before mineral identification, slowing the processing pipeline. 
This paper presents a cross-attention transformer that predicts a mineral-class probability distribution directly from uncorrected top-of-atmosphere (TOA) reflectance, providing a post-hoc surrogate at inference time.
The transformer architecture treats each pixel spectrum as a wavelength-indexed sequence and uses learned query vectors to pool spectral evidence across bands. 
Two physics-infusion extensions are evaluated: 1) spectroscopic priors, implemented as per-head attention-bias initialization from diagnostic wavelengths or USGS reference spectra, and 2) statistical-invariance regularization, implemented as a consistency loss under label-preserving illumination and spectral-slope perturbations. 
The 8-head spectroscopic prior configuration improves validation accuracy relative to the 8-head transformer-only baseline, while the lower-capacity 4-head configuration recovers comparable accuracy with reduced training cost.
Attention analysis shows that the model emphasizes visible and near-infrared regions associated with expected Fe$^{3+}$ absorption structure. 
Cross-region experiments reveal a tradeoff: spectral priors improve accuracy, interpretability, and rare-class behavior (especially at constrained capacity), whereas statistical-invariance regularization's effect is concentrated in rare-class sensitivity. 
These results demonstrate that physics-informed transformer architectures can provide efficient, interpretable surrogates for elements of hyperspectral mineral-retrieval pipelines, improving product latency.

\section{Introduction}

Mineral dust suspended in the atmosphere is one of the largest sources of uncertainty in regional climate radiative-forcing predictions \cite{ipcc_2021}.
Light-colored airborne minerals scatter sunlight, while iron-bearing minerals absorb it and warm the atmospheric column; 
current climate models rely on uncertain estimates of iron-oxide abundance and can produce radiative-forcing predictions that vary by up to 460\% across plausible compositions \cite{emit_l1b_atbd}.
NASA's Earth Surface Mineral Dust Source Investigation (EMIT) was launched in 2022 to reduce this uncertainty by mapping arid-region surface mineralogy, producing approximately 1.6-million-pixel granules across 285 spectral bands.
Translating this data stream into operational mineral-identification products is therefore both a scientific and computational challenge.

The standard mineral-identification pipeline applied to EMIT and similar imaging-spectroscopy missions is a multi-stage sequence of per-pixel operations.
Operational implementations rely on optimization-based schemes such as the Imaging Spectrometer Optimal Fitting (ISOFIT) inversion, often accelerated by neural-network RTM emulators \cite{thompson2018, brodrick2021}, before downstream mineral identification is performed.
Beyond the per-pixel computational cost, the staged architecture imposes operational latency.
EMIT mineral products are delivered with a two-month median latency from acquisition \cite{emit_l2b_atbd}, reflecting downlink, ingest, queueing, atmospheric correction, mineral matching, quality control, and archive production.
For petabyte-scale missions or time-sensitive applications such as volcanic-ash mapping, post-fire debris characterization, and dust-storm response, this operational delivery cycle becomes a constraint and burden separate from the cost of per-pixel computation.

This paper proposes an alternative paradigm: \emph{post-hoc direct retrieval with physics-informed supervision} (Figure~\ref{fig:posthoc_workflow}, Discussion).
Rather than executing the RTM-based atmospheric-correction and spectral-matching chain at inference time, a learned model is trained on a comparatively small set of previously processed L1B--L2B pairs and then used as a low-latency surrogate for the operational L1B-to-mineral ID mapping.
Recent work shows that a pure 1D spectral sequence model, treating each pixel as a wavelength-indexed sequence without spatial context, is sufficient to reach state-of-the-art accuracy on EMIT classification tasks~\cite{lee2025spectf}, supporting the per-pixel formulation adopted here.
The model does not produce an atmospherically corrected reflectance product, nor does it claim independent mineral truth;
its supervision is inherited from the operational science-data-system labels.
Its value is instead operational and methodological: after the one-time training cost, inference requires only a single forward pass from top-of-atmosphere (TOA) reflectance to mineral class.
This enables fast screening or mineral-label prediction without invoking the RTM during deployment.

This direct TOA formulation is challenging because the model must infer mineralogical structure from spectra in which the surface signal is mixed with atmospheric scattering, absorption, transmission, illumination geometry, and sensor effects.
A purely data-driven model may learn useful spectral structure, but it may also exploit scene-specific shortcuts that do not transfer across regions.
The question addressed here is therefore not simply whether a transformer can classify EMIT spectra, but how physical knowledge should enter an attention-based model trained on operational remote-sensing products.
This question is studied through two physics-infusion mechanisms: one architectural and one loss-based.

The physics-augmented cross-attention transformer presented here draws on the PINN and the TGDS machine-learning literature \cite{karpatne2017tgds, raissi2019pinn}.
Spectroscopic knowledge enters through the per-head attention bias $\bm{\beta}_h$, which can be initialized from known diagnostic wavelengths or from principal components of EMIT-resampled USGS reference spectra.
Statistical-invariance knowledge enters through a consistency loss adapted from Vapnik \cite{vapnik2020}, which encourages predictions to remain stable under TOA perturbations representing illumination scaling and spectral-slope variation.
These mechanisms act as soft physical guidance rather than hard physical constraints.
They do not impose a PDE, solve the radiative-transfer equation, or assume that TOA reflectance equals surface reflectance.
Instead, they provide controlled ways to test how spectroscopic priors and statistical-invariance assumptions affect a learned post-hoc retrieval, physics-informed surrogate.

The approach is demonstrated on EMIT TOA hyperspectral observations for 95 Group~1 mineral classes, but the approach is sensor agnostic.
The transformer learns relevant spectral structure directly from data, with attention concentrating near visible and near-infrared regions associated with iron-oxide absorption.
Spectral-prior initialization improves accuracy, interpretability, and capacity efficiency, with the improvement magnitude depending on head count and prior construction methodology; 
statistical-invariance regularization's effect is concentrated in rare-class sensitivity rather than uniformly improving accuracy.
Together, these results position physics-informed spectral transformers as a practical mechanism for accelerating and interpreting the hyperspectral mineral-identification pipeline.

\section{Results}

Four- and eight-head transformer baselines were trained on 1.26~million class-capped TOA pixels from 396 EMIT granules collected over Africa and the Middle East.
The transformer-only baselines used derivative tokenization, learned query vectors, and zero-initialized trainable attention biases, but no spectroscopic prior and no statistical-invariance regularization.
Two physics-injection mechanisms were evaluated against these baselines:
(1) spectroscopic priors, implemented as per-head attention-bias initialization from either diagnostic wavelengths or USGS reference spectra, and
(2) statistical-invariance regularization, implemented as a consistency loss under label-preserving illumination and spectral-slope perturbations.
Combined prior-plus-regularization configurations were trained at $H = 4$ and $H = 8$ to test whether the mechanisms stack or interfere.
Each configuration was evaluated on a held-out Africa/Middle East validation pixel pool, 3 full in-region granules ($\sim$1.5M pixels each) and on 20 cross-region granules from a geographically disjoint southwestern US (SW US) desert region, to assess generalization beyond the training distribution.
Top-1 accuracy is the fraction of mineral pixels whose ground-truth label matches the model's top prediction;
Top-3 accuracy is the fraction whose ground-truth label appears in the top three.

\paragraph{Spectral priors lift accuracy at constrained capacity but not at saturated capacity.}
At $H = 4$, only four learned query vectors compete to cover the 285-band spectrum.
The unguided transformer reaches $0.789$ top-1; the manual and PCA-curated priors raise this to $0.808$ and $0.809$ ($+1.9$ / $+2.0$~pp).
At $H = 8$, the unguided transformer reaches $0.819$ on its own, a $+3.0$~pp gain from the doubled attention capacity, while the manual prior falls to $0.806$ ($-1.3$~pp below the 8H baseline) and the PCA-curated prior ties the baseline at $0.818$.
The pattern is a \emph{capacity-saturation} effect: a 4-head model has too few heads to find the diagnostic Fe$^{3+}$ structure on its own and benefits from a spectroscopic head-start; an 8-head model has enough heads to discover comparable structure unaided.

\paragraph{Spectral priors learn faster from the first epoch.}
Figure~\ref{fig:earlyepoch} shows the first-five-epoch convergence trajectories.
At $H = 4$ the manual prior reaches $0.331$ top-1 after a single epoch versus $0.306$ for the unguided baseline.
The spectral prior's accuracy advantage at $H = 4$ is established at initialization rather than built up during training, while LUSI-like configurations lag in early-epoch top-1.

\paragraph{A smaller model with a spectroscopic prior matches the performance of a larger model without one.}
A 4-head transformer initialized with the manual spectral prior reaches top-1 accuracy $0.808$, within $1.1$~pp of an 8-head unguided transformer ($0.819$).
This almost recovers the $+3.0$~pp gain that doubling the head count would otherwise deliver (Trans $H = 4$: $0.789 \rightarrow$ Trans $H = 8$: $0.819$), while using half as many attention heads and $13\%$ less training time.
Initializing the four available heads at physically plausible Fe$^{3+}$ wavelengths gives the model a spectroscopic head-start that partially substitutes for the broader spectral coverage of a larger architecture.

\paragraph{Attention concentrates near known Fe$^{3+}$ diagnostic wavelengths.}
Figure~\ref{fig:trans4_attn} shows class-conditional mean attention for goethite (class~2, $N = 141$ validation pixels) at $H = 4$ across four parameter-matched configurations.
On the held-out validation split, the transformer baseline reaches F1-score $0.92$ for goethite and $0.93$ for hematite (a second ferric-iron endmember; analogous attention plot in the SI Appendix).

At $H = 4$, the manual and PCA-curated priors produce statistically significant head-averaged Spearman correlation with hematite and goethite absorption depth ($\rho \approx 0.17$--$0.24$), with per-head correlations reaching $\rho \approx 0.39$ ($p < 10^{-7}$) where the prior anchors attention near canonical Fe$^{3+}$ regions.
The unguided $H = 4$ transformer and LUSI-like configurations show no significant alignment.
At $H = 8$, the unguided transformer reaches $\rho = 0.32$ ($p < 10^{-6}$), recovering spectroscopic alignment without explicit initialization.

\paragraph{Spectral priors improve rare-class capture; the LUSI-like loss alone does not.}
When per-class F1 is stratified by training-set support, the manual and PCA-curated priors produce positive median F1 gain in every rarity bin.
In the rare-but-learnable regime of $\sim$200--2000 training pixels per class, the manual prior at $H = 4$ reaches mean F1 $= 0.62$, statistically tied with the $H = 8$ transformer-only baseline (F1 $= 0.64$) and well above LUSI 4H (F1 $= 0.49$).
Above $\sim$1000 training samples per class, all configurations converge to F1 $\approx 0.82$--$0.86$ with negligible separation.
Spectral priors therefore behave as sample-efficiency tools concentrated in the rare-class regime.

\paragraph{Spectral priors and the LUSI-like consistency loss do not stack additively.}
At $H = 4$, combining either spectral prior with the LUSI-like loss is consistently worse than the prior alone on every metric.
PCA-curated + LUSI reaches top-1 $0.790$ ($-1.9$~pp vs PCA-curated alone), and Manual + LUSI reaches $0.795$ ($-1.3$~pp vs Manual alone); both are statistically tied with the unguided baseline.
At $H = 8$ the conflict softens to neutrality, with Manual + LUSI ($0.808$) essentially tied with Manual alone ($0.806$).
The two mechanisms therefore target different performance axes: spectral priors improve in-distribution accuracy, interpretability, and rare-class behavior, whereas the LUSI-like loss's effect is concentrated in rare-class macro recall on out-of-distribution scenes (see Cross-region deployment, below).

\paragraph{Granule-scale deployment within the training region.}
Across three representative Africa/Middle East granules spanning approximately $1.4$--$1.6$~million mineral pixels each, the mean $H = 4$ top-1 ordering is PCA-curated ($0.888$) $>$ Manual ($0.882$) $>$ Transformer ($0.874$) $>$ LUSI ($0.865$); at $H = 8$ the ordering is PCA-curated ($0.892$) $>$ Trans ($0.890$) $>$ Manual ($0.877$) $\geq$ LUSI ($0.874$).
Macro recall favors spectral priors on every granule; on one granule, class~32 reaches F1 $= 0.32$ under the PCA-curated prior versus F1 $= 0.04$ under the transformer baseline, despite having only 117 mineral pixels.

\paragraph{Cross-region deployment to the southwestern US.}
Cross-region deployment to a geographically disjoint southwestern US desert region (20 mineral-populated granules) reduces top-1 accuracy by $8$--$17$~pp from the training-region validation pool, while top-3 accuracy stays in the $0.87$--$0.92$ range across all transformer configurations (Table~\ref{tab:crossscene}).
At $H = 4$ the manual prior leads cross-region with SW top-1 $= 0.724$, $+5.5$~pp above the unguided baseline ($0.669$) and $+9.0$~pp above the LUSI-like configuration ($0.634$); its in-region accuracy of $0.808$ drops by $8.4$~pp under cross-region deployment, the smallest drop in the 4-head ablation.
At $H = 8$ the PCA-curated prior leads (SW top-1 $= 0.705$, an $11.3$~pp drop from in-region $0.818$), narrowly above Manual 8H ($0.699$) and well above LUSI 8H ($0.666$).
On macro recall, however, the LUSI-like configuration's per-granule advantage is the cleanest signal in the cross-region set: LUSI 4H exceeds the unguided $H = 4$ baseline's macro recall on $19$ of the $20$ SW US granules, and exceeds the manual prior's macro recall on $12$ of $20$, even while losing to both on top-1.

\section{Discussion}

The central result of this work is not that every physics-informed intervention improves every metric.
The experiments show that attention architectures provide a controlled way to test \emph{how} physical knowledge enters a learned remote-sensing surrogate, introducing a transparent source of physical information that bridges scientific understanding and machine learning.

\paragraph{What the attention analysis does and does not explain.}
The attention maps provide spectroscopic evidence that the model emphasizes wavelength regions consistent with iron-oxide discrimination, including visible Fe$^{3+}$ charge-transfer structure and VNIR crystal-field regions. 
However, attention weights are not causal explanations by themselves. 
They indicate where each learned query samples the spectrum, while the final class decision also depends on the value vectors $\mathbf{V}_j$, which encode reflectance magnitude, local derivative structure, and band-position information. 
This distinction is particularly important for hematite and goethite, whose diagnostic features overlap substantially. 
The fact that both minerals share attention peaks is therefore not a failure of discrimination; it reflects shared ferric-iron spectral structure. 
The mineral-specific decision is made downstream by the classifier head using the content pooled from those regions. 
Thus, the appropriate interpretive claim is that the learned representation is \emph{consistent with} known spectroscopy, not that attention alone recovers the full physical retrieval logic.

\paragraph{Statistical-invariance regularization reveals a robustness tradeoff.}
The LUSI-like consistency loss was designed to encourage stability under illumination scaling and smooth spectral-slope perturbations, an acknowledged issue in the EMIT pipeline~\cite{emit_l2a_atbd, emit_l2b_atbd}.
The physical motivation comes from the first-order radiative-transfer decomposition linking TOA reflectance to surface reflectance,
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\text{toa}} \approx T_{\downarrow}\,T_{\uparrow}\,\rho_{\text{surface}} + \rho_{\text{path}},
\label{eq:toa_decomp_disc}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\downarrow}T_{\uparrow}$ is the round-trip atmospheric transmittance, $\rho_{\text{path}}$ is the atmospheric path reflectance, and $\rho_{\text{surface}}$ is the Lambertian surface reflectance of the target (the quantity laboratory USGS spectra measure directly)~\cite{vermote1997sixs}.
The two LUSI-like predicates approximate label-preserving directions derived from this decomposition: a multiplicative scale perturbation approximates broadband changes in $T_{\downarrow}T_{\uparrow}$, and a smooth additive spectral-slope perturbation approximates a low-order change in $\rho_{\text{path}}$.

The implementation diverges from Vapnik's original LUSI formulation~\cite{vapnik2020}, in which predicates and statistical invariants enter as \emph{hard constraints} on the admissible function class via minimization of a constrained functional in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS).
The neural training setting does not easily admit hard constraints, so the predicates here enter softly, as a teacher--student consistency loss between predictions on the original spectrum and on its perturbed counterpart.
The empirical results in this paper show that this soft approximation does not improve in-distribution top-1 accuracy and concentrates its benefit in rare-class macro recall on out-of-distribution scenes; exploring the formal hard-constraint implementation is a natural next step.

\paragraph{Post-hoc direct retrieval as a reliable operational surrogate.}
The proposed model should be understood as a post-hoc direct retrieval surrogate for the operational L1B-to-mineral-product mapping. 
Its labels are inherited from the EMIT science data system, and its accuracy is therefore bounded by the quality, thresholds, and assumptions of the mission's SDS atmospheric-correction and spectral-matching pipeline, and not truth. 
Within that scope, the operational advantage is substantial: once trained, the model maps TOA reflectance to mineral labels using a single forward pass and does not invoke an RTM at inference. 
Full-granule experiments show that this inference can be performed on commodity Apple Silicon hardware in tens of seconds per granule. 
A fast surrogate supports rapid ground screening and possible onboard event detection, data downlink prioritization, or exploratory analyses in which approximate mineral labels are useful before validated products are available.

\paragraph{Conditions for transfer beyond EMIT.}
The framework is transferable in structure, not in its specific spectral content: for another sensor, mineral group, or physical-science application, the diagnostic wavelengths, prior construction, and label-preserving transformations would have to be rebuilt from the relevant physics.
What transfers is the recipe --- identify a feature axis with physical meaning, encode prior knowledge as a weak learnable bias over that axis, and evaluate whether loss-based invariances improve robustness to known label-preserving transformations --- making the approach most appropriate for problems where labels are expensive and domain knowledge specifies diagnostic input regions or label-preserving transformations.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations bound the present results.
First, the labels are operational SDS mineral-product labels rather than independent field-validated mineral truth, so the model may inherit that pipeline's biases.
Second, the main ablation results are single-seed runs at one training-set scale and on a single cross-region deployment set (southwestern US); additional seeds, training-size sweeps, and deployment regions are needed to quantify statistical variability and broader spatial generalization.
Third, attention maps and softmax outputs are diagnostic rather than causal or calibrated; ablation/counterfactual tests and post-hoc calibration would be needed to prove that specific bands drive individual decisions and to support probabilistic deployment.
Fourth, the LUSI-like perturbations capture only low-order label-preserving effects (multiplicative brightness, smooth slope) and do not represent the full atmospheric radiative-transfer process.

\paragraph{Future work.}
The next step is to convert the surrogate into a validated operational aid through independent validation against field/laboratory-confirmed mineral targets, cross-sensor testing, evaluation across aerosol optical depth, and surface-brightness regimes, considering post-hoc uncertainty calibration against operational confidence measures.
A second direction is adaptive physics infusion: instead of fixing manual or PCA priors, the model could learn when to trust, attenuate, or override a prior based on scene conditions.
A third direction is causal interpretability of the attention patterns: band-masking, counterfactual spectral editing, and attention-bias intervention experiments could determine which of the highlighted wavelengths are \emph{necessary} for correct classification, moving the attention story from \emph{consistent with} known spectroscopy to a stronger causal claim.

\section{Materials and Methods}

\begin{figure}[!htbp]

The forward pass is partitioned into four regions: preprocessing, tokenization, attention pooling, and classification.
Spectral priors enter through the per-head attention bias $\bm{\beta}_h$; statistical-invariance regularization requires a second (student) forward pass through the network on a transformed input $T_{\text{joint}}(\mathbf{w})$, with the resulting prediction compared against the teacher prediction via a KL consistency loss.}
\label{fig:architecture}

The Materials and Methods are organized to mirror the end-to-end architecture in Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}: the four numbered subsections below correspond to the four shaded modules of the base forward pass: preprocessing, tokenization, attention pooling, and classification, followed by the two optional physics-infusion mechanisms (spectral-prior initialization of the attention bias and LUSI-inspired statistical-invariance consistency regularization) that operate on top of that pass.

\paragraph{(1)~Data and preprocessing.}
Training and validation use a class-balanced sample of $1{,}260{,}203$ EMIT TOA pixels drawn from 396 granules over Africa and the Middle East.
Each pixel is paired with the Group~1 mineral label assigned by the operational EMIT mineral-identification pipeline, which applies Tetracorder-style spectral matching to the pipeline's atmospherically corrected product~\cite{clark2003tetracorder}; the labels are therefore treated as operational science-data-system products rather than independent mineral ground truth.
The original Group~1 distribution is highly imbalanced and dominated by iron-bearing minerals, especially goethite and nano-hematite variants.
To reduce dominance by the most common classes, a stratified per-class cap of $7{,}500$ pixels is applied; the sampled dataset is then split 90/10 into training and validation sets using a fixed random seed.
Labels with value zero are treated as 'non-minerals' and excluded before training.

Each pixel is represented by its $L = 285$-band EMIT top-of-atmosphere (TOA) reflectance vector $\mathbf{w} \in \R^{L}$, robust-z-score normalized using per-band medians and interquartile ranges computed from the training split only:
\[
\hat{w}_j = \frac{w_j - \mathrm{median}(\mathbf{w})}{\mathrm{IQR}(\mathbf{w}) + \epsilon}.
\]
Robust z-score is preferred over mean--standard-deviation normalization because the per-band reflectance distribution is heavy-tailed and contaminated by atmospheric absorption residuals at the EMIT band edges, where outliers could dominate the per-band statistics.

\paragraph{(2)~Tokenization and embedding.}
The normalized spectrum is tokenized using a shape-aware spectral representation that concatenates each band value with its first and second finite differences along wavelength, following the derivative-spectroscopy motivation of Tsai and Philpot~\cite{tsai1998derivative}: $\mathbf{u}_j = [\hat{w}'_j,\, \Delta\hat{w}_j,\, \Delta^2\hat{w}_j]^T \in \R^{3}$.
Each spectral token is then embedded via a learned linear layer into a content embedding $\mathbf{c}_j \in \R^{d/2}$, concatenated with a learned positional embedding $\mathbf{p}_j \in \R^{d/2}$, and passed through a linear mixing layer with LayerNorm to produce a token embedding $\mathbf{e}_j \in \R^{d}$ with $d = 192$.
The token embedding sequence forms the matrix $\mathbf{E} \in \R^{L \times d}$.

\paragraph{(3)~Attention pooling.}
The model uses $H$ learned query vectors $\mathbf{q}_h \in \R^{d}$ to cross-attend to the $L$ spectral tokens, following the asymmetric cross-attention construction of the Perceiver~\cite{jaegle2021perceiver} that replaces self-attention with a small set of learned queries and reduces complexity from $O(L^2)$ to $O(H \cdot L)$.
Per-head key and value matrices are linear projections $\mathbf{K} = \mathbf{E}\mathbf{W}_K$ and $\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{E}\mathbf{W}_V$.
For head $h$, the attention score vector is
\[
\mathbf{s}_h = \frac{\mathbf{K}\mathbf{q}_h}{\sqrt{d}} + \bm{\beta}_h,
\]
where $\bm{\beta}_h \in \R^{L}$ is a learnable per-head spectral bias.
The attention weights are
\[
\mathbf{A}_h = \mathrm{softmax}\left(\frac{\mathbf{s}_h}{\tau_a}\right),
\]
where $\tau_a = 0.9$ is the attention temperature.
The pooled head representation is $\mathbf{z}_h = \mathbf{V}^{T}\mathbf{A}_h$, and the $H$ pooled vectors are concatenated into $\mathbf{z}_{\text{cat}} = \mathbf{z}_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus \mathbf{z}_H \in \R^{Hd}$.
In the transformer-only baseline, $\bm{\beta}_h$ is initialized to zero but remains trainable, parameter-matching the physics-prior configurations so that any improvement from the prior is attributable to initialization rather than added trainable parameters.

\paragraph{(4)~Classification.}
The pooled concatenation $\mathbf{z}_{\text{cat}}$ is passed through a two-layer MLP head ($Hd \to d \to Q_1$) with ReLU activation and dropout $p = 0.1$, producing logits over $Q_1 = 95$ Group~1 mineral classes.
The predicted mineral identity is $\hat{q}_1 = \arg\max \mathrm{softmax}(\mathrm{logits})$; the corresponding softmax distribution provides the top-1 and top-3 retrievals reported throughout this paper.
The softmax output is treated as a model probability rather than a calibrated posterior probability.

\paragraph{Physics infusion (optional): spectroscopic attention-bias initialization.}
Spectroscopic prior information is injected by initializing $\bm{\beta}_h$ before training.
The bias is not a hard constraint; after an optional short freeze period, it is updated by gradient descent like the rest of the model, so the prior acts as a weak inductive starting point that the model can preserve, attenuate, amplify, or reshape.
Two prior constructions are evaluated, summarized below. In both, the bias is initialized as $\bm{\beta}_h^{(0)} = \alpha\,\mathbf{b}_h$, with $\mathbf{b}_h \in \R^{L}$ the per-head prior profile and $\alpha = 1.0$ the scalar prior strength.

\subparagraph{Manual prior.}
Literature-derived Fe$^{3+}$ diagnostic wavelengths are encoded as Gaussian bumps over wavelength.
The default manual prior uses five visible and near-infrared ferric-iron regions: $480$, $535$, $670$, $860$, and $920$~nm, corresponding to goethite and hematite visible features and broader Fe$^{3+}$ crystal-field absorption structure~\cite{Jiang2014AlGoethite, clark1999, sherman1985, burns1993}.
Each center wavelength $\lambda_c$ is represented as
\[
b(\lambda;\lambda_c,\sigma) = \exp\left[-\frac{(\lambda - \lambda_c)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right],
\]
with $\sigma = 25$~nm.
The Gaussian bumps are assigned to heads by round-robin allocation and peak-normalized to form $\mathbf{b}_h$.

\subparagraph{PCA-curated prior.}
The prior is constructed offline from a curated subset of the USGS Spectral Library~\cite{kokaly2017usgs}, with each laboratory spectrum convolved to EMIT's 285 bands.
The subset includes three categories: \emph{targets} (hematite, goethite, and iron-bearing variants --- the classes the model must identify), \emph{spectroscopic confusers} (other Fe-bearing minerals whose spectra overlap the targets, ensuring the prior captures within-family variability), and \emph{hard negatives} (Fe-poor minerals such as chlorites and pyroxenes whose spectra could be confused with iron oxides under noise, the borderline cases the model must learn to reject).
Spectra are sanitized to remove fill values and non-finite entries, small missing gaps are interpolated along wavelength, and spectra with excessive missingness are excluded, leaving $\sim$$56$ surviving spectra in the reference matrix $\mathbf{X}_{\mathrm{ref}}$.
Each spectrum is row-normalized to suppress broadband-albedo variation and emphasize spectral shape; principal components are computed over the valid non-water-vapor bands ($\sim$$220$ of $285$), expanded back to the full 285-band EMIT grid, and converted into attention-bias profiles using z-scored absolute loading magnitudes:
\[
\beta_h(\lambda) = z\left(|v_h(\lambda)|\right),
\]
where $v_h(\lambda)$ is the signed loading vector for principal component $h$.
The water-vapor absorption regions and the noisy long-wavelength edge are set to zero in the prior.

\paragraph{Physics infusion (optional): LUSI-inspired statistical-invariance consistency loss.}
The loss-based physics injection is a consistency regularizer motivated by Vapnik--Izmailov statistical invariants~\cite{vapnik2020}; its connection to the TOA decomposition (Equation~\ref{eq:toa_decomp_disc}) and the soft-loss relaxation of formal LUSI are discussed above.
For each training pixel, the joint transformation applied to a single-pixel spectrum $\mathbf{w}$ is
\[
T_{\text{joint}}(\mathbf{w}) = \rho\,\mathbf{w} + \xi\,\bm{\lambda}_{\text{grid}},
\]
where $\rho \sim U(0.8, 1.2)$ is a multiplicative brightness scale (approximating broadband variation in the round-trip transmittance $T_{\downarrow}T_{\uparrow}$ in Equation~\ref{eq:toa_decomp_disc}), $\xi \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.01)$ is a spectral-slope coefficient (approximating first-order wavelength dependence of the additive path-reflectance term $\rho_{\text{path}}$), and $\bm{\lambda}_{\text{grid}} \in \R^L$ is a fixed linear ramp from $-1$ to $+1$ across the $L$ bands; $\rho$ and $\xi$ are sampled independently for each pixel at each training step.
Transformations are applied in raw reflectance space: the normalized input is denormalized, perturbed, clamped to a physically valid range, and renormalized before being passed through the model.
The unperturbed prediction $\mathbf{p}_{\text{orig}}$ acts as a stop-gradient teacher and the perturbed prediction $\mathbf{p}_{\text{trans}}$ as the student, and the consistency loss is the temperature-scaled Kullback--Leibler divergence
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KL}}
= D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(\mathbf{p}_{\text{orig}}^{\mathrm{sg}}\;\|\;\mathbf{p}_{\text{trans}}\right),
\]
where the stop-gradient on $\mathbf{p}_{\text{orig}}$ prevents both predictions from drifting toward a shared trivial output.
The total objective is $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{LUSI}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KL}}$, with $\lambda_{\mathrm{LUSI}}=1$ when enabled.

\paragraph{Optimization and ablation protocol.}
Models are trained for 120 epochs using AdamW with peak learning rate $5\times10^{-4}$, cosine decay, 5\% linear warmup, batch size 1{,}024, and cross-entropy loss with label smoothing $0.05$~\cite{szegedy2016rethinking}. 
The attention-bias parameters are placed in a separate optimizer group with zero weight decay. 
For prior-bearing models, the initialized prior heads are frozen for a short initial period and then released for standard gradient-based training. 
For transformer-only and LUSI-like-only baselines, the attention bias is initialized at zero and learned from data.

All ablation runs shown in Table \ref{tab:crossscene} use the same data split, random seed, hardware, optimizer settings, and data pipeline.
Wall-clock timings are measured on a single Apple M4 Max laptop using PyTorch's MPS backend, with full training plus validation taking 117--326 minutes per configuration.
Configurations that include the LUSI-like consistency loss approximately double the wall-clock cost of those that do not, because each training step performs an additional augmented forward pass.
At inference time, full-granule prediction on an approximately $1.6$-million-pixel EMIT granule takes $\sim$$28$~$\mu$s per pixel) on the same hardware, independent of the prior-construction choice.
These timings are intended to compare configurations under identical workstation conditions, not to represent an optimized high-performance-computing deployment.
