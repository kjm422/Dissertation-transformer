\section*{Significance Statement}
\noindent
Mapping Earth's surface mineralogy from orbiting hyperspectral sensors is critical for understanding mineral dust's role in climate, but the standard pipeline rejects the very high-aerosol pixels it was designed to study because the underlying radiative transfer inversion fails.
We show that a cross-attention transformer trained directly on uncorrected top-of-atmosphere reflectance replaces this multi-step pipeline with a single forward pass, recovering canonical iron-oxide absorption features through interpretable attention.
The approach realizes physics-informed deep learning for an attention-based architecture: spectroscopic priors enter through attention bias initialization, and statistical invariants drawn from atmospheric transfer enter through consistency regularization.
The result is a transferable framework for injecting domain knowledge into transformer models in any physical-science setting where labeled data is bounded by an upstream physical model.

\section*{Abstract}
\noindent
Hyperspectral satellite missions like NASA's Earth Surface Mineral Dust Source Investigation (EMIT) map the mineralogy of arid regions, but operational pipelines depend on computationally expensive radiative transfer model (RTM) inversion to atmospherically correct each pixel before mineral identification --- a bottleneck that becomes prohibitive at petabyte scale and rejects the very high-aerosol pixels these missions were designed to study.
We present a cross-attention transformer that classifies minerals directly from uncorrected top-of-atmosphere (TOA) reflectance, bypassing RTM inversion at inference.
Two physics-injection mechanisms complement the architecture: spectroscopic priors derived from laboratory reference libraries are pinned to attention heads via the per-head bias term, and a consistency-loss regularizer based on Vapnik--Izmailov statistical invariants enforces that predictions remain stable under physically motivated illumination and atmospheric perturbations.
Together, the two mechanisms realize the architectural-prior and loss-based-prior ingredients of physics-informed deep learning in an attention-based model.
A 4-head transformer with a manual spectral prior derived from canonical Fe$^{3+}$ diagnostic wavelengths matches the accuracy of an 8-head transformer-only baseline at 14\% lower training cost, demonstrating that physics-informed initialization substitutes for architectural capacity.
Consistency-loss regularization is the only configuration that maintains accuracy on a geographically disjoint test scene where unregularized configurations degrade --- a property consistent with PINN observations that physics-driven regularization improves extrapolation to physics-respecting test conditions.
The framework opens a path to recovering hyperspectral data currently discarded at high aerosol loads, where dust mass loading is highest and conventional pipelines fail.

\section{Introduction}

Mineral dust suspended in the atmosphere is one of the largest sources of uncertainty in regional climate radiative forcing predictions \cite{ipcc_2021}.
Light-colored airborne minerals such as quartz scatter sunlight, while iron-bearing minerals such as hematite absorb it and warm the atmospheric column; current climate models rely on highly uncertain estimates of iron-oxide abundance and produce radiative-forcing predictions that vary by up to 460\% across plausible compositions.
NASA's Earth Surface Mineral Dust Source Investigation (EMIT) \cite{emit_l1b_atbd} was launched in 2022 to constrain this uncertainty by mapping arid-region surface mineralogy from the International Space Station, producing approximately one million pixels per orbit across 285 spectral bands.
Translating this petabyte-scale data stream into precise mineralogical maps is a defining computational challenge of the mission.

The standard mineral-identification pipeline applied to EMIT and similar missions is a sequence of two computationally intensive per-pixel operations: atmospheric correction via radiative transfer model (RTM) inversion, followed by spectral-library matching against a curated set of laboratory reference spectra.
Operational implementations rely on optimization-based schemes such as the Imaging Spectrometer Optimal Fitting (ISOFIT) inversion combined with neural-network RTM emulators \cite{thompson2018, brodrick2021}, which together perform an iterative retrieval at every pixel.
Beyond the per-pixel computational cost, the pipeline imposes a physical limit: pixels with aerosol optical depth (AOD) exceeding 0.5 at 550~nm are systematically rejected because the underlying RTM inversion fails to converge reliably under heavy aerosol loading \cite{emit_l2a_atbd, emit_l3_atbd}.
This rejection is operationally most acute over dust-source regions --- the very regions EMIT was designed to study --- where atmospheric dust loading routinely exceeds the threshold.
The result is a circular operational constraint: atmospheric correction is most needed where it is most likely to fail.

We propose an alternative paradigm: \emph{post-hoc direct retrieval}, in which a deep model learns the mapping from L1B top-of-atmosphere (TOA) reflectance to mineral classification directly, using a small set of pixels already processed by the operational pipeline as supervision.
The model never invokes the RTM at inference; the iterative atmospheric-correction step is replaced by a single forward pass, and pixels rejected by the operational pipeline at high AOD remain accessible to the trained model.
This shift trades a one-time training cost (paying the full pipeline price on a small subset of granules) for low-marginal-cost inference at petabyte scale.
The architecture must, however, accommodate the absence of atmospheric correction during inference --- meaning it must learn to identify mineralogical features from TOA spectra in which the surface signal is convolved with atmospheric scattering, transmission, and absorption.
The contribution of the present work is to show how physics-informed deep learning techniques, originally developed for partial-differential-equation residuals \cite{raissi2019pinn} and theory-guided machine learning \cite{karpatne2017tgds}, transfer to this task.

We present a cross-attention transformer that is physics-informed in the sense of Karpatne et al.\ and Raissi et al.\ \cite{karpatne2017tgds, raissi2019pinn}: spectroscopic priors are injected through the attention bias $\bm{\beta}_h$, and statistical invariants drawn from atmospheric transfer enter through a consistency loss adapted from Vapnik and Izmailov \cite{vapnik2020}.
The two mechanisms realize the two foundational ingredients of physics-informed deep learning --- architectural prior initialization and physics-driven loss regularization --- in an attention-based architecture, made natural and inspectable by the attention bias's role as an explicit physics-injection interface.
We demonstrate this on EMIT TOA hyperspectral observations: a 4-head transformer with a manual spectral prior matches the accuracy of an 8-head transformer-only baseline at 14\% lower training cost, and consistency regularization is the only configuration that maintains accuracy on a geographically disjoint test scene where unregularized configurations degrade.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Cross-attention transformer recovers Fe\texorpdfstring{$^{3+}$}{3+} diagnostics from TOA reflectance}

The architecture (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}) consists of a cross-attention transformer with $H$ learned query vectors that probe an $L = 285$-band spectral token sequence, producing per-head attention weights $\mathbf{A}_h \in \R^L$ that can be inspected as spectroscopic relevance scores.
We trained an $H = 4$ baseline on 1.26~million class-balanced TOA pixels from 396 EMIT granules over Africa and the Middle East with no physics-informed initialization ($\bm{\beta}_h = \mathbf{0}$, frozen) and no consistency regularization, and evaluated per-class attention on two iron-oxide endmembers from the USGS Spectral Library: hematite (GDS27) and goethite (WS222 medium-grain).

Quantitative validation by Spearman rank correlation between attention magnitude and per-mineral USGS absorption depth confirms the visual reading. Architectural priors achieve statistically significant per-mineral alignment (PCA-manual head-avg $\rho = 0.238$ for hematite, $p < 10^{-3}$; $\rho = 0.202$ for goethite, $p < 10^{-2}$; PCA-curated $\rho = 0.216$, $0.170$ respectively). Per-head, the strongest cells are striking: \emph{PCA-manual Head~1 reaches $\rho = 0.389$ ($p < 10^{-8}$) on hematite} with top peaks at 530/544/559~nm and 873~nm matching the canonical 535~nm visible charge-transfer and 860~nm NIR crystal-field; \emph{PCA-manual Head~0 reaches $\rho = 0.394$ ($p < 10^{-7}$) on goethite} with top peaks at 470/485 and 902~nm matching the canonical 480~nm charge-transfer and 920~nm crystal-field. The unguided baselines do not reach significance per mineral (transformer baseline hematite $\rho = 0.059$, n.s.; LUSI $\rho = 0.070$, n.s.) --- evidence that without spectroscopic guidance, attention is too diffuse over non-mineral-specific wavelengths to align with per-mineral absorption patterns at the head-averaged level. At $H = 8$ this ranking flips: the unguided transformer baseline reaches $\rho = 0.320$ ($p < 10^{-6}$) on hematite --- the same alignment level the manual prior produces at $H = 4$, achieved through head-doubling --- while PCA-manual collapses to $\rho = 0.038$ (n.s.) with multiple per-head correlations becoming \emph{anti-correlated} as the trainable bias drifts during the post-freeze phase. The architectural prior's accuracy gain is concentrated in the rare-class regime: stratifying per-class F1 by training-set support shows Manual~4H reaching F1 = 0.62 in the 150--1000 sample bin (statistically tied with Trans~8H at F1 = 0.64 and well above LUSI~4H at F1 = 0.49), while at the common-class end (1000--4000 samples) all configurations converge to F1 = 0.82--0.86 --- the canonical PINN-tradition pattern that priors help most where training data is scarce. The per-head attention pattern for goethite is nearly identical to that for hematite, since the model attends to similar Fe$^{3+}$ regions for spectroscopically nearby minerals; discrimination happens in the value vectors $\mathbf{V}_j$ that encode the actual reflectance content at each attended band, not in the attention weights.


\subsection{Two complementary physics-injection mechanisms}

\subsubsection{Architectural prior: attention-bias initialization substitutes for capacity}

The attention bias $\bm{\beta}_h$ provides an explicit physics-injection interface (yellow ``spectral priors'' box, Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}).
Two independent constructions of $\bm{\beta}_h$ were evaluated, each distilling USGS Spectral Library knowledge through a different abstraction: a \emph{manual} mode in which Gaussian bumps are pinned at literature-published Fe$^{3+}$ diagnostic wavelengths (480, 535, 670, 860, 920~nm); and a \emph{PCA-curated} mode in which absolute principal-component loadings on a hand-curated subset of the library are z-score-normalized over the active wavelength range, producing a bias profile with positive peaks at PCA-discovered diagnostic regions and a slight negative floor at off-diagnostic bands.

The headline finding is that the manual spectral prior at $H = 4$ delivers $1.9$ percentage points of the $3.0$~pp gain that doubling head count would otherwise produce (Trans $H = 4$: $0.789 \to$ Trans $H = 8$: $0.819$, Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) --- approximately $63\%$ of the head-doubling benefit at zero additional training time, and a useful operating point when training compute is constrained. The PCA-curated prior at $H = 4$ reaches $0.809$ top-1 from a largely non-overlapping band set, supporting an interpretation in which the architectural prior contributes \emph{capacity substitution} rather than \emph{wavelength selection}: the model benefits from any spectroscopically structured initialization at $H = 4$, not from a specific choice of canonical bands. The transformer baseline at $H = 4$ uses a trainable attention bias initialized at zero, parameter-matched to the prior-bearing configurations, so the prior's $+1.9$~pp gain is unambiguously attributable to the spectroscopic initialization itself rather than to added trainable parameters or differential preprocessing.
At $H = 8$, the two architectural priors diverge: the \emph{manual} prior underperforms the parameter-matched transformer baseline by $-1.3$~pp ($0.819 \to 0.806$) because its $5$ narrow Gaussian bumps over-constrain attention at saturated capacity, while the \emph{PCA-curated} prior reaches $0.818$ (statistically tied with the baseline) because its diffuse loadings across $\sim$220 bands align with the variance directions the unguided model discovers anyway. Together these results refine the standard physics-informed-deep-learning observation \cite{raissi2019pinn, karpatne2017tgds}: priors help most when capacity is constrained, and at saturated capacity the harm depends on how concentrated the prior's spectral support is --- sharp priors over-constrain, diffuse priors are neutral.

\paragraph{Architectural and loss-based priors do not stack at constrained capacity.}
The combined PCA-curated~+~LUSI run at $H = 4$ reaches top-1 $0.790$ (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) --- $1.9$~pp \emph{below} PCA-zabs alone and statistically tied with the unguided baseline. The trainable bias amplified to $\|\bm{\beta}\|_{\text{rms}} = 1.648$ in this run, well above either prior alone (PCA-zabs: $0.946$; LUSI alone: $1.308$); LUSI's consistency loss appears to use the bias as a buffer between augmented and un-augmented passes, distorting the structured PCA initialization. The two mechanisms target different failure modes (architectural priors for in-distribution accuracy; loss-based priors for cross-region robustness) and conflict rather than stack at constrained capacity. A corresponding $H = 8$ combined run was not evaluated under the current parameter-matched pipeline; the legacy 8-head PCA+LUSI configuration ($0.805$, statistically tied with legacy 8-head PCA-only at $0.806$) is consistent with the conjecture that the interference dissipates at saturated capacity.

Diagnostic measurements during training corroborate the capacity-substitution interpretation.
The attention-bias RMS magnitude $\|\bm{\beta}\|_{\text{rms}}$ at the end of training was $0.977$ for the manual run (essentially unchanged from its initialization at $\alpha = 1.0$) and $0.946$ for the PCA-curated run --- both within the regime in which the model held the prior near its initial scale rather than washing it out.
At $H = 8$ the manual run instead amplified the prior to $\|\bm{\beta}\|_{\text{rms}} = 1.154$ during training, indicating that the eight-head model leaned more heavily on the prior-anchored heads rather than discovering complementary features through its three additional random heads.
Average pairwise cosine similarity between heads' learned query vectors was $0.015$ at $H = 8$ versus $0.031$ at $H = 4$ (Manual configurations), ruling out head collapse as an explanation for the $H = 8$ saturation: the random heads stayed distinct from the prior-anchored heads but the model elected not to use that capacity for novel feature discovery.


\subsubsection{Loss-based prior: consistency regularization improves cross-region generalization}

The consistency-loss regularizer (yellow ``LUSI'' box, Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}) penalizes prediction drift under two physically motivated transformations of each input: a global multiplicative scaling representing illumination changes from topographic shading, and an additive linear tilt across wavelength representing residual aerosol-induced spectral slope distortion.
Both transformations are label-preserving for mineral identification by construction.

\subsection{Together: post-hoc direct retrieval at high atmospheric loading}

The two mechanisms are complementary by design.
The architectural prior keeps in-distribution accuracy high (4-head with prior matches 8-head transformer-only); the loss-based prior keeps out-of-distribution accuracy stable across atmospheric conditions distinct from training.
Together they support the central operational claim of post-hoc direct retrieval: a single forward pass on uncorrected TOA reflectance can produce mineral classifications across the full range of atmospheric conditions, including the AOD~$> 0.5$ regime where the operational pipeline systematically fails.

\section{Materials and Methods}

The architectural overview is in Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}; the four shaded regions correspond to the standard transformer pipeline stages (data preprocessing, tokenization, attention pooling, classification), and the two yellow side-blocks correspond to the two physics-injection mechanisms described below. Full architectural equations, hyperparameters, sanitization pipeline, training protocol, and an extended ablation table including LUSI and legacy-PCA configurations are provided in SI Appendix.

\paragraph{Cross-attention transformer (Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}).}
Each pixel's $L = 285$-band TOA reflectance is robust-z-score normalized using per-band median and IQR computed on the training split, and tokenized via a shape-aware encoding that concatenates raw reflectance with first and second finite differences along $\lambda$ \cite{tsai1998derivative}.
$H$ learned query vectors $\mathbf{q}_h \in \R^d$ each compute scaled dot-product attention against the $L$ token positions, producing per-head attention weights $\mathbf{A}_h \in \R^L$ via a softmax with temperature $\tau_a$.
Pooled head outputs $\mathbf{z}_h = \mathbf{V}^\top \mathbf{A}_h$ are concatenated and passed through a two-layer MLP to produce class logits over $Q_1 = 95$ Group-1 mineral classes.
The distinguishing architectural feature is the additive attention bias $\bm{\beta}_h \in \R^L$, which appears in the per-head attention score $\mathbf{s}_h = \mathbf{K}\mathbf{q}_h / \sqrt{d} + \bm{\beta}_h$: when $\bm{\beta}_h = \mathbf{0}$ the model is a standard cross-attention transformer; when $\bm{\beta}_h$ is set from spectroscopic information, attention is steered toward physically meaningful wavelengths before training.

\paragraph{Architectural prior: $\bm{\beta}_h$ initialization.}
The bias is initialized in one of two modes.
In the \emph{manual} mode (default), the user supplies a list of literature-derived diagnostic wavelengths $\lambda_c$ (default Fe$^{3+}$ list: 480, 535, 670, 860, 920~nm).
Each wavelength is encoded as a Gaussian bump $b(\lambda; \lambda_c, \sigma) = \exp\!\left[-(\lambda - \lambda_c)^2 / (2\sigma^2)\right]$ with $\sigma = 25$~nm, and bands are distributed across heads via round-robin assignment.
Each head's bumps are normalized to peak amplitude one and then scaled by the prior strength $\alpha = 1.0$, giving a peak attention-logit bias of $+1$ at each head's assigned diagnostic wavelength and zero elsewhere --- a deliberately gentle nudge rather than a hard constraint.
In the \emph{PCA-curated} mode, $\bm{\beta}_h$ is constructed offline from PCA on a hand-curated subset of the USGS Spectral Library (hematite/goethite targets, ferric-oxide confusers, and non-iron-oxide hard negatives), with per-spectrum sanitization and row-z-score preprocessing applied before SVD; the absolute principal-component loadings, z-score-normalized per head, are saved as an $(H, 285)$ array and loaded at training start.
In both modes, the prior-bearing heads are frozen for the first 3 epochs to ensure the model uses the bias as an inductive starting point rather than an immediate gradient target.

\paragraph{Loss-based prior: consistency regularization.}
The consistency-regularization loss is computed by sampling, for each training pixel, two physically motivated transformations of its TOA reflectance: a global multiplicative scaling $\rho \sim U(0.8, 1.2)$ representing illumination changes from topographic shading, and an additive linear tilt $\xi \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.01)$ across wavelength representing residual aerosol-induced spectral slope distortion.
The transformations are applied in raw reflectance space (with denormalization, perturbation, clamping to physically valid range, and renormalization), and the model's prediction on the perturbed input is compared to its prediction on the unperturbed input via Kullback--Leibler divergence with temperature $\tau_l = 2$.
Following common consistency-regularization practice, gradients propagate only through the perturbed-input pass; the unperturbed pass acts as a stop-gradient teacher signal.
The total training loss is $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + \lambda_{\text{LUSI}}\, \mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}}$ with $\lambda_{\text{LUSI}} = 1$.

\paragraph{Training data.}
Training and validation use a class-balanced sample of 1{,}260{,}203 EMIT L1B TOA pixels drawn from 396 granules covering Africa and the Middle East.
Each pixel is supervised with the Group-1 mineral class assigned by the operational EMIT mineral-identification pipeline (Tetracorder \cite{clark2003tetracorder}, applied to the pipeline's atmospherically corrected L2A product); the supervision label is bounded by the AOD threshold of the operational pipeline.
The class distribution is highly imbalanced (Tetracorder labels are dominated by goethite and nano-hematite variants); we apply a stratified per-class cap of 7{,}500 pixels and split 90/10 for training and validation with a fixed random seed.

\paragraph{Optimization.}
Models are trained for 120 epochs with AdamW (base learning rate $5 \times 10^{-4}$, cosine decay with 5\% linear warmup, batch size 1{,}024).
Cross-entropy loss with label smoothing $0.05$ \cite{szegedy2016rethinking} is the primary objective; consistency loss adds the term described above when enabled.
All wall-clock timings are measured on a single Apple M4 Max laptop (40-core GPU) using PyTorch's MPS backend; the absolute training+validation budgets reflect what a single consumer workstation can deliver, not an HPC environment.
Relative comparisons across configurations remain valid because every result reported uses the same hardware, the same fixed random seed, and an identical data pipeline.

\paragraph{Cross-region evaluation.}
Cross-region performance is evaluated by training on the Africa/Middle East dataset and evaluating on a geographically disjoint southwestern US desert scene (Mojave/Sonora), using only pixels with valid Group-1 labels and AOD~$\le 0.5$ in both regions.
Three configurations are compared at $H = 8$: transformer-only (no physics injection), spectral-prior (PCA-curated, no continuum removal), and consistency-loss-regularized.
Reported numbers are the percentage-point change in top-1 accuracy relative to the same model's in-distribution Africa/Middle East validation accuracy.
